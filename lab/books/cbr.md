# Preface

For most of human history, reality has been understood through the dominant technology of the age.

Ancient civilizations understood reality through mythology.

The mechanical age understood reality as machinery.

The industrial era understood reality as production.

The information age understands reality as information.

As artificial intelligence emerges and computational systems become increasingly capable, a new question naturally arises:

> What if reality itself is computational?

This book explores a framework called Compute-Based Reality (CBR), a speculative but potentially testable model that views physical existence as an information-processing architecture.

The proposal is not that reality merely resembles computation.

The proposal is that computation may be fundamental.

Within this framework, gravity, time, entropy, quantum phenomena, and even consciousness may emerge from deeper informational and computational structures.

The objective of this work is not to prove such a theory.

Instead, it is to ask whether such a theory can be formulated scientifically.

Can it generate predictions?

Can it be falsified?

Can it explain existing observations?

Can it predict something that current theories do not?

These are the standards that separate philosophy from science.
